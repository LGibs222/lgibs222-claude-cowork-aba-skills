# Teacher Coaching Note

## Audience
General education or special education teacher implementing a behavior plan. The note is collaborative — peer-to-peer in tone, even when the consultant has more BCBA-level training.

## Output structure

1. **Greeting** — first name, single sentence acknowledging the observation context.

2. **What I saw working** — exactly 2 specific moments. Concrete, observable, named by what the teacher did. Not "great job!" but "I noticed when J. started to escalate during transition, you used the visual schedule to walk him through the next 10 minutes — that's exactly the antecedent strategy in his BIP."

3. **One thing to try next** — exactly ONE coaching point. Tied to the BIP / plan. Specific enough that the teacher knows what to do differently tomorrow.

4. **What I'll do to support** — 1 sentence. What the consultant will bring (data sheet, modeled session, materials).

5. **Check-in** — when the consultant will be back, or how the teacher can reach them.

## Tone rules
- Collaborative, peer-to-peer. Never evaluative.
- "Let's try" beats "you should."
- Always lead with what worked. Never open with the coaching point.
- Specific over generic. "Great job with the student" is banned. Name the moment.
- Use the teacher's language for the strategies when they have one (if they call it the "calm corner," call it the "calm corner," not "antecedent-based regulation space").

## Required content
- One coaching point. Not three. Teachers cannot implement three changes at once between observations.
- Tie the coaching point to a specific element of the BIP or plan if one exists.
- Include a concrete next step (visit date, materials, a session you'll model).

## Ask before drafting
- Teacher's first name and grade/role.
- Student initials.
- Date and length of observation.
- 1–3 specific moments worth naming as "working."
- The single highest-leverage coaching point (from the consultant's clinical judgment).
- Whether there's an active BIP/plan to reference, and what intervention element the coaching point ties to.
- When the next check-in is.

## Do not
- Use language that sounds like evaluation ("needs improvement," "did not adhere to fidelity").
- Include a list of corrections — one coaching point only.
- Send a generic note that doesn't reference specific moments. Specificity is what makes coaching land.
- Reference the student's behavior in stigmatizing terms in a teacher-facing document — model the language you want them to use.

## Format
Email body length, plain prose, no Markdown headings unless requested. Aim for 150–250 words total. The teacher should be able to read it in under a minute and know exactly what to try tomorrow.
